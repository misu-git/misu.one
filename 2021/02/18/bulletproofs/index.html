Bulletproofs

Bulletproofs are among the distinct Zero-knowledge Proof systems

Like the Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-Interactive ARguments of Knowledge (zk-SNARK), the Zero Knowledge Prover and Verifier for Boolean Circuits (ZKBoo) and the Succinct Transparent ARgument of Knowledge (STARK).

Zero-knowledge proofs are designed to allow a prover to indirectly ensure that a statement is true without any information after the statement has been verified.

In order to demonstrate that a number is found to be used to resolve a cryptographic puzzle and that the hash value is adjusted without the disclosure of the hash value.

The Bulletproofs Technology is a non-interactive zero-knowledge (NIZK) protocol with very short proofs for general arithmetic circuitry (Arguments of Knowledge Systems).

They rely on the assumption of Discrete Logarithm (DL) and are interactive with the Fiat-Shamir Heuristic.

The name “Bulletproof” came from a nontechnical summary by one of the original authors of the scheme: “Short like the bullet with security assumptions that prove to be ballots.”

Bulletproofs is essentially its algorithm for internal products, originally presented by Groth and further refined by Bootle et la.

Later development provided evidence (knowledge argument) for two independent (not related) binding vector Pedersen Commitments, which met the relationship between the product and product.

Bulletproofs build upon these techniques, which provide no-knowledge, communication effective evidence, but replace the internal product argument with an additional one, reducing communication overall by three.

Bulletproofs are also deployed in a Multi-Party Computing (MPC) protocol that aggregates distributed evidence of several provers with secret committed values into a single proof before the challenge of Fiat Shamir is determined and transmitted to the verifier and minimizes communication rounds.

Secret values committed will remain secret.
